## Intervention

- All patients underwent HVPG measurement with IV propranolol response assessment
- **Responders** (HVPG decrease >= 10%): randomised to **propranolol** (up to 160 mg BID) vs placebo
- **Non-responders**: randomised to **carvedilol** (up to 25 mg/day) vs placebo
- Doses individually determined during open-label titration period
- Randomisation 1:1 via centralised web-based system

---

## Primary Endpoint

Incidence of **cirrhosis decompensation** (ascites, variceal bleeding, or overt hepatic encephalopathy) **or death**.

Analysis: ITT with deaths unrelated to the liver treated as competing events.

## Safety

- Overall incidence of adverse events similar between groups
- 6 patients with severe adverse events (4 in beta-blocker group, 2 in placebo)
- Adherence adequate in 82% (beta-blocker) and 83% (placebo)

---

## Key Conclusions (from abstract)

> "Long-term treatment with beta blockers could increase decompensation-free survival in patients with compensated cirrhosis and CSPH, mainly by reducing the incidence of ascites."

---

## Clinical Significance

- **First RCT** to demonstrate that non-selective beta-blockers prevent hepatic decompensation in compensated cirrhosis with CSPH
- The benefit was primarily driven by **reduction in ascites**
- Led to recommendations in **Baveno VII** (2022) for NSBB use in CSPH even without high-risk varices
- Inspired follow-up studies: BOPPP trial (UK, carvedilol in small varices), Chinese RCT (carvedilol stratified by liver stiffness)
- Nested cohort studies from PREDESCI also demonstrated impact of bacterial infections on decompensation risk and metabolomics for risk prediction
